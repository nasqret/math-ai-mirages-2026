# The Mirages and Twists of Mathematics with AI

This book is the text-friendly, source-grounded companion to the lecture at
MIM UW on 22 June 2026. It preserves the mathematics, methodological caveats,
speaker notes, executable examples, and provenance that do not fit on slides.

**Author:** Bartosz Naskręcki<br>
**Affiliations:** Adam Mickiewicz University, Poznań; Centre for Credible AI,
Warsaw University of Technology<br>
**Lecture:** 50 minutes plus discussion

The governing distinction is simple: **a fluent mathematical answer, a correct
informal proof, a formally checked theorem, and a new mathematical idea are four
different achievements.**

## The thesis

The capability jump of early 2026 is real. Long tool-using runs, formal proof
search, code generation, and stateful research workbenches are materially more
useful than the chat systems of even a year earlier. The popular explanation is
nevertheless wrong when it turns isolated successes into general mathematical
autonomy.

The most defensible current successes have a trace:

1. a versioned statement;
2. an inspectable proof or program;
3. an explicit tool and compute budget;
4. independent criticism or replay; and
5. a human who owns the mathematical claim.

This companion therefore separates public evidence, provider-reported evidence,
first-person observations, and forecasts. Private competition details and
confidential benchmark problems are intentionally not reproduced.

## How to use this book

- Chapters 1–2 separate claims from proof checking.
- Chapters 3–4 describe agentic research loops and concrete projects.
- Chapters 5–6 treat evaluation, compute, access, and governance.
- Chapters 7–8 give a bounded forecast and exact replay instructions.

The slides are designed for oral argument. This book is designed for checking.
